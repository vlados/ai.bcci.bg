Yes—but we should **generate analysis, not invent evidence**.

The strongest model is:

> **EU-wide secondary research + original Bulgarian primary research + transparent derived analysis.**

That gives the Council both credibility and originality. The EU research establishes the benchmark; the Bulgarian survey produces the unique data that journalists, institutions, companies, search engines, and AI systems may cite.

# 1. What we can legitimately produce

## A. EU desk research

We can collect and analyse existing data from:

- Eurostat.
- European Commission Digital Decade reports.
- European Commission AI Office.
- Joint Research Centre.
- European Investment Bank surveys and reports.
- National statistical institutes.
- Official national AI strategies and implementation documents.

This can become a publication such as:

# **AI Adoption in European Business 2026: Bulgaria in the EU Context**

It would not be an original EU-wide survey. It would be an **independent analysis of official EU data**. That distinction should be stated clearly.

## B. Original Bulgarian research

The Council can conduct an original survey among Bulgarian companies, supplemented by interviews and case studies.

This produces evidence that does not currently exist in sufficient detail:

- Which AI tools Bulgarian companies actually use.
- Which business processes use AI.
- Whether AI produces measurable cost savings or revenue.
- Whether companies have internal AI policies.
- Whether employees receive AI literacy training.
- Whether companies understand their AI Act obligations.
- Which industries are adopting fastest.
- What prevents adoption.
- What support companies expect from BCCI, government, universities, and technology providers.

## C. Derived indicators

We can calculate new metrics from official and original data, for example:

- Bulgaria–EU adoption gap.
- AI maturity index.
- AI governance readiness score.
- AI literacy readiness score.
- Sector opportunity score.
- AI Act preparedness index.
- Adoption-to-productivity conversion rate.
- Percentage of “informal AI” users versus governed enterprise deployments.

These would be our analytical constructs, not official EU indicators. Each formula and weighting must be published.

# 2. There is already a strong EU research story

The latest Eurostat data provide a useful starting point.

In 2025, approximately **20% of EU enterprises with at least 10 employees used AI technologies**. Bulgaria’s rate was **8.55%**, placing it among the three lowest EU countries, alongside Poland and Romania. Bulgaria was therefore **11.4 percentage points below the EU average**, with adoption equal to approximately 43% of the EU rate.

That produces a defensible opening finding:

> **Bulgaria’s business AI adoption rate is less than half the EU average, despite accelerating adoption across Europe.**

The adoption gap is also strongly connected to company size. Across the EU in 2025:

| Enterprise size | Using AI |
|---|---:|
| Small enterprises | 17.0% |
| Medium enterprises | 30.36% |
| Large enterprises | 55.03% |

This means large enterprises were more than three times as likely as small enterprises to report AI use.

The barriers are also measurable. Among EU enterprises that had considered AI but decided not to use it:

- Approximately 71% cited insufficient expertise.
- Approximately 53% cited unclear legal consequences.
- Approximately 49% cited data-protection and privacy concerns.
- Only around 21% said AI was not useful for their enterprise.

That is strategically important. The evidence suggests that the primary problem is not that companies see no value in AI. The larger obstacles are **capability, legal uncertainty, data governance, and implementation capacity**.

There is also a broader population and workforce dimension. In 2025, 32.7% of people aged 16–74 in the EU reported using generative AI tools, compared with 22.5% in Bulgaria. Bulgaria was therefore approximately 10.2 percentage points below the EU average.

These four findings alone can support a credible initial research landing page.

# 3. Recommended flagship research structure

## Proposed title

# **AI Adoption and Readiness in European Business 2026**
## **Bulgaria in the EU Context**

The report should contain three distinct evidence layers.

## Part I — EU-27 benchmark

This section would use official European datasets to analyse:

1. Overall business AI adoption.
2. Adoption by country.
3. Adoption by company size.
4. Adoption by economic sector.
5. Types of AI technology used.
6. Business functions where AI is used.
7. Reasons for non-adoption.
8. Digital skills and AI literacy.
9. Cloud, data analytics, and digital-intensity prerequisites.
10. Regulatory and investment environment.

A particularly useful analytical question would be:

> Is Bulgaria’s low AI adoption mainly an AI problem, or is it the result of weaker underlying digitalisation, cloud adoption, data maturity, skills, and management capacity?

That is much more valuable than publishing a simple country ranking.

## Part II — Original Bulgarian business survey

The Bulgarian survey should answer questions that Eurostat does not answer in sufficient operational detail.

### Module 1: Company profile

- Industry.
- Region.
- Number of employees.
- Revenue band.
- Export activity.
- Ownership.
- Local or international group membership.
- Digital maturity.
- Internal IT capacity.

### Module 2: Actual AI adoption

The questionnaire must distinguish among:

- No AI use.
- Individual employees using public AI tools informally.
- Approved use of generative AI tools.
- AI embedded in existing software.
- Custom internal AI systems.
- AI integrated into core business processes.
- AI-based products or services sold to customers.

This distinction is essential. A company where one employee uses ChatGPT is not equivalent to a company with AI integrated into its operations.

### Module 3: Use cases

Measure use in:

- Marketing and content.
- Sales.
- Customer support.
- Finance and accounting.
- Legal work.
- Human resources.
- Software development.
- Operations.
- Manufacturing.
- Logistics.
- Procurement.
- Cybersecurity.
- Research and product development.
- Strategic decision-making.

### Module 4: Business impact

Ask whether companies have observed:

- Reduced operating costs.
- Faster task completion.
- Increased revenue.
- Improved customer service.
- Better decisions.
- Reduced errors.
- New products or services.
- Lower staffing requirements.
- Increased demand for higher-skilled employees.
- No measurable impact yet.

Do not ask only whether AI is “useful.” Ask whether the company measures its effect and how.

### Module 5: Barriers

Include comparable Eurostat-style options:

- Lack of relevant skills.
- Legal uncertainty.
- Privacy and data protection.
- Cybersecurity concerns.
- Poor internal data quality.
- Integration costs.
- Lack of management support.
- Uncertain return on investment.
- Employee resistance.
- Lack of trusted suppliers.
- Intellectual-property concerns.
- Customer or reputational risk.
- AI is not relevant to the company.

Using some of the same wording as Eurostat will allow direct comparison between Bulgaria and EU results.

### Module 6: AI governance

Measure whether the business has:

- An inventory of AI tools.
- An approved AI policy.
- Restrictions on confidential-data entry.
- Human-review requirements.
- Vendor assessment.
- Risk classification.
- Incident reporting.
- Output-verification procedures.
- Copyright rules.
- Data-retention rules.
- Responsible executive or working group.

### Module 7: AI literacy and AI Act readiness

Article 4 of the EU AI Act has applied since 2 February 2025 and requires providers and deployers to take measures appropriate to the knowledge, experience, and context of people working with AI systems. Supervision and enforcement provisions begin applying in August 2026.

The survey should therefore ask:

- Are employees trained to use AI?
- Which employee groups receive training?
- Is training technical, legal, ethical, or operational?
- Does the organization maintain training records?
- Has the company identified whether it is a provider, deployer, importer, or distributor?
- Has it assessed whether any systems may be high-risk?
- Does management understand prohibited practices?
- Is there a person responsible for AI governance?

This would make the research highly timely and commercially useful.

### Module 8: Desired support

Ask companies what they need:

- Basic AI training.
- Management training.
- AI Act guidance.
- Legal templates.
- Technology-vendor recommendations.
- Readiness assessments.
- Funding information.
- Access to infrastructure.
- Sector-specific examples.
- University partnerships.
- Pilot-project support.
- Cybersecurity and privacy guidance.

This section can directly determine the Council’s future educational and policy programme.

# 4. Sampling: the most important methodological issue

An open questionnaire distributed through BCCI mailing lists and social networks can produce valuable findings, but it should not automatically be called a **nationally representative survey**.

An open survey has self-selection bias:

- Digitally active companies are more likely to respond.
- Companies interested in AI are more likely to respond.
- BCCI members may differ from the total enterprise population.
- Large and internationally oriented firms may be overrepresented.
- Microbusinesses may respond differently from larger companies.

## Defensible survey options

### Option 1 — Stakeholder survey

Distribute the survey openly through:

- BCCI members.
- Partner organizations.
- Universities.
- Sector associations.
- Regional chambers.
- Social channels.

Label the result accurately:

> “Survey among Bulgarian business representatives and BCCI stakeholders.”

This is useful for understanding attitudes and identifying cases, but it should not be presented as nationally representative.

### Option 2 — Structured national business survey

Use a defined sampling frame and stratify responses by:

- Company size.
- NACE sector.
- Region.
- Export activity.
- Enterprise age.

A target of approximately **600 completed responses** gives a theoretical maximum sampling margin of roughly ±4 percentage points for an uncomplicated random sample. Approximately **1,000 completed responses** reduces that to around ±3.1 percentage points. Real business surveys may have a larger effective error because of weighting, clustering, non-response, and sampling design.

For meaningful sector-by-size comparisons, 1,000 responses may still be insufficient unless key groups are deliberately oversampled.

### Option 3 — Hybrid model

This is probably the best practical design:

1. A structured sample for defensible national indicators.
2. An open stakeholder survey for reach and engagement.
3. Twenty to thirty expert interviews.
4. Ten to fifteen verified company case studies.

The structured and open survey results must be kept separate. Combining them without methodological controls would contaminate the representative sample.

# 5. A critical issue with the EU comparison

Eurostat’s primary enterprise AI indicators generally cover enterprises with **at least 10 employees** in specified parts of the non-financial business economy. They do not represent every company, every sector, every public institution, or the large number of Bulgarian microenterprises.

Therefore, we should not write:

> “Only 8.55% of Bulgarian companies use AI.”

The more accurate formulation is:

> “In 2025, 8.55% of Bulgarian enterprises with at least 10 employees in the sectors covered by Eurostat’s ICT enterprise survey reported using at least one AI technology.”

For readability, the main article can use a shorter statement, but the methodology note must preserve the complete definition.

The Bulgarian survey could intentionally include microenterprises. However, results for companies with fewer than 10 employees would then need to be reported separately because there is no directly equivalent Eurostat EU benchmark.

# 6. Comparison groups

Comparing Bulgaria only with the EU average is too simplistic. The study should use several predetermined comparison groups.

## EU average

Shows Bulgaria’s overall distance from the European benchmark.

## Regional peers

Possible group:

- Romania.
- Greece.
- Croatia.
- Hungary.
- Poland.
- Slovakia.
- Czechia.
- Slovenia.

The final group should be justified by geography, economic structure, business size distribution, or historical digital performance—not selected after seeing which comparison makes Bulgaria look favourable.

## Adoption leaders

Eurostat’s 2025 leaders include Denmark, Finland, and Sweden. Their business AI adoption rates were approximately 42.0%, 37.8%, and 35.0%, respectively.

They show what mature adoption looks like, but direct comparison must acknowledge major structural differences.

## Aspirational structural comparators

Countries such as Portugal or Estonia may be useful because their digital-development paths can offer more applicable lessons than simply comparing Bulgaria with the largest Western European economies.

## AI Adoption Funnel

A maturity funnel could classify companies as:

1. Unaware.
2. Aware but uninterested.
3. Interested but blocked.
4. Experimenting informally.
5. Piloting.
6. Operationally deploying.
7. Scaling.
8. Creating AI products or services.

Eurostat primarily measures whether specified technologies are used. The Bulgarian survey can measure where companies are in the adoption process.

## Shadow AI Indicator

Measure companies where employees use AI without:

- Formal approval.
- Training.
- Security controls.
- Data rules.
- Vendor assessment.
- Management visibility.

This could become one of the study’s most newsworthy indicators.

## Value Realization Index

Separate:

- AI use.
- AI implementation.
- AI measurement.
- Measurable business effect.

Many reports incorrectly treat adoption as proof of productivity. It is not. Our research should test whether AI use produces measurable value.

## Governance Gap

Compare:

- Percentage using AI.
- Percentage with an AI policy.
- Percentage with employee training.
- Percentage maintaining an AI inventory.
- Percentage measuring risks.
- Percentage measuring financial outcomes.

A likely—but currently unproven—hypothesis is that adoption is advancing faster than governance. The survey would test it rather than assume it.

## AI Readiness Index

Possible dimensions:

| Dimension | Example weight |
|---|---:|
| Strategy and management | 20% |
| Data and infrastructure | 20% |
| Skills and literacy | 20% |
| Governance and compliance | 20% |
| Operational adoption and measurement | 20% |

However, composite indices are inherently subjective. They should always be accompanied by the individual component scores and full scoring methodology.

# 9. Initial research hypotheses

The survey can test hypotheses such as:

1. Bulgarian AI adoption is constrained more by skills and implementation capacity than by lack of perceived usefulness.
2. Informal employee use of generative AI is substantially higher than formally approved enterprise use.
3. Large companies have adopted AI faster, but smaller companies may use public generative AI tools more informally.
4. Companies use AI mainly for text, marketing, and administrative work rather than core production processes.
5. Most companies using AI do not systematically measure return on investment.
6. AI governance and employee training lag behind actual tool use.
7. Export-oriented and internationally owned firms have higher adoption and stronger governance.
8. Legal uncertainty is more significant in regulated sectors.
9. Data quality and integration become stronger barriers as companies move from experimentation to operational deployment.
10. Bulgarian businesses need practical templates and sector-specific guidance more than general awareness campaigns.

These must be labelled as hypotheses until tested.

That combination would give the website three distinct forms of value:

> **We may create original calculations, classifications, indexes, interpretations, forecasts, and recommendations—but every underlying fact must be sourced, and every original Bulgarian statistic must come from a documented research method.**

The sensible next step is to design the Bulgarian questionnaire around Eurostat-compatible core variables while adding the deeper governance, ROI, generative-AI, and AI Act questions that make the Council’s research genuinely original.
